Function

The function of a power supply is to convert AC power into DC power.

Behavior

The input section consists of a transformer.  It converts the AC input voltage into AC voltage that more closely approximates the required DC voltage.

For many purposes, a DC voltage with a slight ripple is sufficient, so the output of the power supply is taken directly from the output of the filter.  However, there are three defects with this kind of DC voltage.  First, there is a slight ripple.  Second, when the voltage of the AC power changes, then the DC voltage changes.  Third, when the load on the power supply changes substantially, the DC voltage can change.  To fix all three problems, the power supply can have a voltage regulation section.  This uses a zener diode or similar device to keep the output voltage at a constant value despite changes in the AC power input and the load.  It also removes the ripple.


If the output voltage is constant, then as the load resistance decreases, the current increases.   When the load resistance gets too low or even becomes zero (a short), too much current is drawn from the power supply and its components can be damaged.  To prevent this, a protection section can monitor the current and shut down the power supply when too much current flows.  The simplest protection section is just a fuse.  However, one has to physically replace to fuse in order to use the power supply again.   More complex protection circuitry can reset itself when the load resistance rises to acceptable levels.
